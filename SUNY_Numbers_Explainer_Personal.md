## The 60-Second Version

Community colleges have a graduation problem. Nationally, only **34 out of 100** students who start at a community college graduate within 3 years. At SUNY community colleges, it's even worse — **26 out of 100**.

A program called **ASAP** was invented at CUNY (the New York City system) in 2007 that basically wraps students in support — free textbooks, subway passes, dedicated advisors, tutoring, money for emergencies. It nearly **doubled** graduation rates in a gold-standard scientific study.

Governor Hochul is now rolling out ASAP across SUNY (the rest of New York state), spending **$20M/year** and targeting **10,000 students at 44 campuses** by Fall 2026. Early results are good but not as good as CUNY's. The pitch: **Google Cloud builds the data infrastructure that closes that gap.**

## The Numbers That Matter (and What They Mean)

### The Graduation Rate Landscape

| Who | 3-Year Grad Rate | What This Means |
|-----|-----------------|-----------------|
| **SUNY community colleges** | ~26% | Only 1 in 4 SUNY CC students graduate in 3 years. This is bad. |
| **National average (all CCs)** | ~34% | SUNY is 8 points *below* even this mediocre number. |
| **ASAP students (CUNY)** | 40.1% | ASAP students graduate at *higher* than the national average. |
| **Control group (no ASAP)** | 21.8% | Similar students who didn't get ASAP — shows ASAP's real effect. |

**Where these numbers come from:**
- SUNY ~26%: Center for an Urban Future report, "Struggling to the Finish Line" (2017), using IPEDS data
- National ~34%: National Student Clearinghouse, "Completing College" report, 2020 starting cohort
- ASAP 40.1% vs 21.8%: MDRC randomized controlled trial (the gold standard — they randomly assigned students to ASAP or not, then tracked outcomes)

**Key thing to understand:** The "3-year graduation rate" for community colleges is actually measured at **150% of normal time** (a 2-year degree measured at 3 years). This is the standard IPEDS metric everyone uses. So when we say "3-year graduation rate," we mean "did they finish their associate degree within 3 years."

### ASAP's Effect Size — Why People Care

The MDRC study found ASAP increased graduation by **+18.3 percentage points** (40.1% vs 21.8%). Here's why that's a big deal:

- MDRC has run **30+ randomized controlled trials** of community college programs
- **ASAP's effect is the largest of ANY of them** — nothing else comes close
- An RCT is the same methodology used in drug trials — it's the highest standard of evidence in social science
- This isn't a survey or a before/after comparison — it's a genuine scientific experiment

### The ROI — Columbia University Study

In 2013, economists Henry Levin and Emma Garcia at Columbia University (Teachers College) did a formal cost-benefit analysis of ASAP. The study is literally titled "Benefit-Cost Analysis of Accelerated Study in Associate Programs (ASAP)."

Here's what they found:

| Number | What It Means in Plain English |
|--------|-------------------------------|
| **$3.50 per $1 (taxpayer)** | For every dollar the government spends on ASAP, taxpayers get $3.50 back in higher tax revenue, lower social services costs, lower criminal justice costs, etc. |
| **$12.20 per $1 (student)** | For every dollar the student "invests" (their time, foregone wages), they get $12.20 back in lifetime earnings. |
| **$46.5M per 1,000 students** | The total net benefit to society for every 1,000 students who go through ASAP (vs. not going through it). This includes *everything* — earnings, taxes, health, crime reduction. |
| **$6,500 lower cost per graduate** | Even though ASAP costs more upfront per student, because so many more graduate, the cost *per graduate* is actually $6,500 cheaper. Think of it like: spending $5 each on 10 lottery tickets where 4 win vs. spending $3 each on 10 tickets where only 2 win — the "winning ticket" is cheaper in the first scenario. |

**Citation:** Levin, H.M. & Garcia, E. (2013). Center for Benefit-Cost Studies of Education (CBCSE), Teachers College, Columbia University. [Full PDF here](https://www.cuny.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/4/page-assets/home-preview/asap/evaluation/Levin_ASAP_Benefit_Cost_Report_FINAL_05222013.pdf). Also published as a journal article in *The Journal of Higher Education* in 2017.

## The SUNY-Specific Story

### What's Happened So Far

SUNY adopted a version of ASAP (they call it **ASAP|ACE** — ACE is "Accelerated Completion and Engagement," the 4-year version):

| When | How Many Students | How Many Campuses |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Fall 2024 | 4,270 | 25 |
| Fall 2025 | 7,050 | 34 |
| Fall 2026 (target) | 10,000 | 44 |

State investment: **$20M/year** (FY26-27 budget).

### SUNY's Early Results (These Are Preliminary)

- **17 pp** first-to-second-term retention gain (ASAP students vs. non-ASAP)
- **15 pp** one-year retention gain
- **73% vs. 61%** credit completion (ASAP vs. non-ASAP) — that's a 20% improvement
- **89% vs. 82%** credit completion for ACE (4-year) students
- **80% vs. 72%** spring-to-fall persistence
- **500+ students on the waitlist** — more demand than capacity

These are "quasi-experimental" — meaning they compared ASAP students to non-ASAP students, but didn't randomly assign them. Still strong, but not as rigorous as an RCT.

### The One RCT at SUNY: Westchester Community College

MDRC did run one genuine RCT at a SUNY campus — Westchester Community College (their program is called "Viking ROADS"). Result: **+11.8 pp** graduation increase (35.5% vs. 23.7%).

That's good! But it's less than CUNY's +18.3 pp and Ohio's +16 pp.

## Quick Answers to Questions Someone Might Ask You

**"Where does the $3.50 ROI number come from?"**
Columbia University (Teachers College). Economists Levin and Garcia, 2013. Formal benefit-cost analysis of ASAP. Published as a journal article in 2017. It's literally called "Benefit-Cost Analysis of ASAP."

**"Is ASAP actually proven, or is this just hype?"**
It's the most rigorously evaluated community college program in history. MDRC (a respected nonpartisan research org) ran a randomized controlled trial — the same methodology used in clinical drug trials. The +18.3 pp graduation effect is the largest ever measured across 30+ such trials.

**"Why is SUNY's effect smaller than CUNY's?"**
Two reasons: (1) SUNY is newer to this — CUNY has had since 2007 to refine their approach, build data systems, train advisors, and iterate. (2) The one SUNY RCT (Westchester) was during COVID, which hurt all outcomes. The gap is real but somewhat inflated by timing.

**"What does Google actually build?"**
A unified data platform (BigQuery) that pulls student data from all campuses into one place. On top of that: predictive models (Vertex AI) that flag at-risk students, dashboards (Looker) so advisors and leaders can see what's happening, and AI tools (Gemini) that help advisors draft outreach, prep for meetings, and manage more students without losing quality.

**"Why not just use EAB or Civitas like other schools?"**
Three reasons: (1) They sell campus-by-campus — SUNY needs system-wide. (2) Their models are black boxes — SUNY can't see or control how predictions are made, and FOIA investigations have found bias issues. (3) We're already there — Phase 1 built the infrastructure, the trust, and the contracts. No ramp-up needed.

**"What's the ask right now?"**
An 8-week no-cost prototype at 2-3 campuses. Zero financial commitment. Google builds it, leaves it running, SUNY evaluates. Then they decide.
